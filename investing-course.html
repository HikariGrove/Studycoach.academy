Master Class

What Ray Dalio Knows

Dalio built Bridgewater Associates into the world's largest hedge fund ($150B+ AUM) using a handful of powerful, repeatable principles. Here's the distilled framework.

🌊
The Economic Machine
Dalio sees the economy as a simple machine with three key drivers: productivity growth (long-term), the short-term debt cycle (5–8 years), and the long-term debt cycle (75–100 years). Most crises happen when debt levels become unsustainable — what he calls "the big debt cycle." Understanding where you are in each cycle is the foundation of macro investing.
  • Credit expansion drives short cycles; watch central bank policy
  • Productivity drives long-run prosperity — technology is the key lever
  • Debt crises are inevitable, predictable, and navigable if prepared
  • Deleveraging happens four ways: austerity, defaults, money printing, wealth transfer
⚖️
Radical Truth & Radical Transparency
Bridgewater's culture operates on ruthless honesty — every meeting is recorded, every decision debated openly, every person rated in real time. Dalio calls this "an idea meritocracy." The goal: eliminate ego and status from decision-making so the best ideas win. For investing, this means writing down your beliefs, stress-testing them publicly, and updating them when wrong.
  • Write down your beliefs so they can be examined and falsified
  • Seek out people who disagree — they see what you can't
  • Separate your ego from your ideas: being wrong is fine, being dishonest is not
  • Build a "Baseball Card" system — track everyone's strengths, weaknesses, and track records
🎯
The Holy Grail of Investing
Dalio's most important investing insight: finding 15 or more uncorrelated return streams is the only free lunch in investing. With 15 uncorrelated assets, you can reduce portfolio risk by ~80% without reducing expected returns. This is the mathematical foundation of the "All Weather" portfolio and most risk-parity strategies.
  • Diversification across uncorrelated assets is more powerful than picking good assets
  • Return/risk ratio improves dramatically with each uncorrelated stream added (diminishing returns after 15)
  • The challenge: true non-correlation is rare — most assets correlate in crises
  • Build a "portfolio of bets" not a "portfolio of positions"
🔁
The Pain-Reflection-Progress Loop
Dalio's formula for personal and professional mastery: Pain + Reflection = Progress. Every loss, mistake, or crisis is a data point. The investor who doesn't learn from losses is doomed to repeat them. Build systematic post-mortems after every significant decision — what did I believe, what happened, why was I wrong, what's the updated model?
  • Keep an investment journal — write down your thesis before every trade
  • Review closed positions: what would you do differently?
  • Don't paper over losses with rationalisations — diagnose the root cause
  • Over 5+ years, pattern-match your systematic errors
🌤️
The All Weather Portfolio — Asset Allocation for Every Environment
Dalio designed the All Weather Portfolio to perform in any macroeconomic environment: rising/falling growth, rising/falling inflation. The four quadrants of the economic machine each favour different assets. The portfolio balances risk (not dollars) across these quadrants.
30% Stocks (Equities) S&P 500, Global Equities
→ Rising growth environment
40% Long-Term Bonds 20–25yr Treasuries
→ Falling growth & inflation
15% Medium Bonds 7–10yr Treasuries
→ Balanced exposure
15% Hard Assets Gold (7.5%) + Commodities (7.5%)
→ Rising inflation hedge
Note: The All Weather Portfolio is designed for stability across all market cycles, not maximum returns. In bull markets, it will underperform a 100% equity portfolio — that is by design. Rebalance annually.
Research Skills

How to Read an Equity Research Report

Firms like Kalkine Group, Goldman Sachs, Morningstar, and JP Morgan publish analyst reports that contain a goldmine of information — if you know how to extract it.

1
Start with the Rating and Price Target — Then Be Sceptical
Every Kalkine-style report opens with a recommendation (Buy / Hold / Sell / Speculative Buy) and a 12-month price target. Read this first, then set it aside. Your goal is to understand the logic behind it, not to blindly follow it. Analyst conflicts of interest are real — sell-side analysts rarely issue Sell ratings because their banks want to maintain corporate relationships.
What to note: Rating · Current price · Price target · Implied upside % · Report date
Red flag: Price target within 5% of current price despite a "Buy" rating
Pro tip: Check the analyst's historical accuracy — many research platforms track this
2
Extract the Core Investment Thesis (2–3 Sentences)
A good research report will state clearly WHY the stock should be bought or avoided. Look for the analyst's key reasons: is it a valuation argument (stock is cheap relative to peers), a catalyst argument (upcoming FDA approval, contract win), or a turnaround argument (cost cutting improving margins)? If you can't state the thesis in 2–3 sentences, the report hasn't made a convincing argument.
Questions to answer: What is the company? Why is it mispriced? What's the catalyst? What's the timeline?
Watch for: Vague language like "well-positioned" without specifics — this is analyst filler
Example thesis: "Kalkine rates XYZ a Buy because trading at 8x EV/EBITDA vs 14x sector average, with margin expansion driven by new automation capex completing in Q2"
3
Interrogate the Financial Model & Key Assumptions
The financial projections are the heart of the report. Look at 3–5 year revenue growth assumptions, EBITDA margin expansion, and free cash flow projections. Every report makes assumptions — your job is to identify the most critical ones and stress-test them. If revenue grows 15% instead of the projected 25%, does the investment thesis still work? Build a simple sensitivity table.
Key metrics to extract: Revenue CAGR · EBITDA margin (current vs projected) · FCF yield · Net debt trajectory · CapEx requirements
Critical assumptions to question: Revenue growth rate · Margin expansion pace · Discount rate / WACC · Terminal growth rate (in DCF)
Bull/base/bear case: Good reports provide 3 scenarios. Read all three — the bear case shows you the downside risk.
4
Analyse the Valuation Methodology
How did the analyst arrive at the price target? Common methods: DCF (discounted cash flow), EV/EBITDA multiple, P/E multiple, Sum-of-the-Parts (SOTP), or dividend discount model. Each method has different inputs and weaknesses. Multiple valuation methods converging on a similar number gives more confidence. If only one method is used, be suspicious.
DCF: Check the WACC and terminal growth rate — small changes = huge price target swings
Comps / multiples: Is the peer group cherry-picked to support the thesis?
SOTP: Used for conglomerates — does the discount or premium to the sum make sense?
Rule of thumb: If the implied upside >50%, either it's a great opportunity or the assumptions are heroic
5
Read the Risks Section — This Is the Most Honest Part
Analysts must disclose risks, and while they tend to bury them at the back, this section often contains the most realistic analysis. Categorise risks: Execution risk (can management deliver?), Market risk (macro headwinds), Regulatory risk (policy changes), Financial risk (debt levels, covenants), Competition risk. Rank which risk is most likely to derail the thesis.
Key risk categories: Execution · Competition · Macro/Rates · Regulatory · Debt/Liquidity · Valuation contraction
Ask: If the #1 risk materialises, what happens to the stock? What's the downside?
Dalio principle: The bear case must not be ruinous. Position size accordingly.
6
Cross-Reference with Primary Sources
Never make an investment decision based solely on a third-party research report. Use the report as a starting point, then verify with primary sources: the company's latest annual report (10-K/20-F), earnings call transcripts, investor day presentations, and SEC filings. Check if management guidance matches what the analyst is projecting. Read competitor filings to validate market share claims.
Primary sources to always check: Annual Report / 20-F / 10-K · Earnings call transcripts (seeking Alpha, Motley Fool) · Investor Day slides · 13F filings (what institutions own) · SEC EDGAR filings
Free databases: EDGAR (SEC), ASIC (Australia), Companies House (UK), SEDAR (Canada)
Kalkine reports often reference ASX-listed companies — verify on ASX announcements platform
7
Form Your Own Independent View
After reading the report and doing your own research, do you agree with the recommendation? Why or why not? Write this down. If you agree, what would have to be true for you to be wrong? If you disagree, what does the analyst know that you don't? Great investors read research to gather data and perspectives — but they always form independent conclusions.
Your investment memo should cover: Do I agree with the thesis? Why/why not? · What am I paying for growth? · What's my margin of safety? · What's my entry price and exit price? · What would change my mind?

Key Terms

The Investor's Glossary

Master these terms and you'll understand 95% of any financial conversation, report, or media coverage.

AUM
Assets Under Management — the total market value of assets a firm or investor manages. The primary size metric for funds.
Alpha
Return above the market benchmark after adjusting for risk. A fund with 5% alpha returned 5% more than explained by market exposure.
Beta
Sensitivity to market movements. Beta of 1.5 means the stock moves 1.5× the market. Higher beta = higher risk and return potential.
EBITDA
Earnings Before Interest, Taxes, Depreciation & Amortisation. A proxy for operating cash flow, used for valuation comparisons.
EV / Enterprise Value
Market cap + net debt. The total takeover cost of a business. Used in EV/EBITDA and EV/Revenue multiples.
DCF
Discounted Cash Flow. A valuation method that projects future cash flows and discounts them back to present value using WACC.
WACC
Weighted Average Cost of Capital. The blended cost of debt and equity used as the discount rate in DCF models.
FCF
Free Cash Flow = Operating Cash Flow minus Capital Expenditure. The actual cash the business generates after maintaining/growing assets.
P/E Ratio
Price-to-Earnings. Share price divided by EPS. Tells you how many years of earnings you're paying. Context-dependent by sector.
Sharpe Ratio
Return per unit of risk (standard deviation). A Sharpe of 1.0 is good, 2.0+ is excellent. Compares managers on risk-adjusted basis.
Short Selling
Borrowing shares and selling them, hoping to buy back cheaper later. Profits if the stock falls. Unlimited downside risk.
Moat
Buffett's term for a durable competitive advantage that protects a company from competition over the long term.
GP / LP
General Partner (fund manager, runs the fund) and Limited Partner (investor in the fund). The core legal structure of most funds.
2 and 20
Standard hedge fund fee: 2% annual management fee on AUM + 20% of profits. Often negotiated down for large investors.
High-Water Mark
The fund's previous peak NAV. The manager only earns performance fees on new profits above this level, protecting investors.
NAV
Net Asset Value. Total assets minus liabilities, divided by units/shares outstanding. The fund's per-unit price.
Drawdown
Peak-to-trough decline. A 20% drawdown means the portfolio fell 20% from its high. Maximum drawdown is the worst historical decline.
13F Filing
US SEC quarterly disclosure of institutional equity holdings. Lets you see what Buffett, Dalio, Ackman etc are buying and selling.
